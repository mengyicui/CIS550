<p><b>GMC</b> (General Motors Truck Company), formally the <b>GMC Division of General Motors LLC</b>, is a division of the American automobile manufacturer <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> (GM) that primarily focuses on trucks and utility vehicles. GMC sells <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Truck_classification" title="Truck classification">commercial</a> trucks, <a href="/wiki/Buses" class="mw-redirect" title="Buses">buses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">vans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_vehicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Military vehicles">military vehicles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">sport utility vehicles</a> marketed worldwide by <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GMC_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/History_of_General_Motors" title="History of General Motors">General Motors</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/William_C._Durant" title="William C. Durant">William C. Durant</a> on September 16, 1908, as a <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> for <a href="/wiki/Buick" title="Buick">Buick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1909, GM purchased the <a href="/wiki/Rapid_Motor_Vehicle_Company" title="Rapid Motor Vehicle Company">Rapid Motor Vehicle Company</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pontiac,_Michigan" title="Pontiac, Michigan">Pontiac, Michigan</a>, forming the basis of the General Motors Truck Company, from which the "GMC Truck" <a href="/wiki/Brand_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Brand name">brand name</a> was derived. (Rapid was established on December 22, 1901, by Max Grabowsky. The company developed some of the earliest commercial trucks ever designed, and utilized one-cylinder engines.) The Reliance Motor Car Company (another independent manufacturer) was also purchased that same year by GM. Rapid and Reliance were merged in 1911, and in 1912 the marque "<b>GMC Truck</b>" first appeared on vehicles exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_International_Auto_Show" title="New York International Auto Show">New York International Auto Show</a>. Some 22,000 trucks were produced that year, though GMC's contribution to that total was a mere 372 units. GMC had some currency within GM referring to the corporate parent in general. Later "GMC" would become distinct as a division brand within the corporation, branding trucks and coaches; in contrast, the abbreviation for the overall corporation eventually ended up as "GM".</p>
<p>GMC maintained three manufacturing locations in <a href="/wiki/Pontiac,_Michigan" title="Pontiac, Michigan">Pontiac, Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Louis, Missouri">Saint Louis, Missouri</a>.</p>
<p>In 1916, a GMC Truck crossed the country from <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> in thirty days, and in 1926, a 2-ton GMC truck was driven from New York to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> in five days and 30 minutes. During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, GMC Truck produced 600,000 trucks for use by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a>.</p>
<p>In 1925, GM purchased a controlling interest in <b><a href="/wiki/Yellow_Coach_Manufacturing_Company" title="Yellow Coach Manufacturing Company">Yellow Coach</a></b>, a bus manufacturer based in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> which was founded by <a href="/wiki/John_D._Hertz" title="John D. Hertz">John D. Hertz</a>. After purchasing the remaining portion in 1943, GM renamed it <b>GM Truck and Coach Division</b>. The Division manufactured interurban coaches until 1980. Transit <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> production ended in May 1987. The Canadian plant (in <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">London, Ontario</a>) produced buses from 1962 until July 1987. GM withdrew from the bus and coach market because of increased competition in the late 1970s and 1980s. Rights to the <a href="/wiki/Rapid_Transit_Series" title="Rapid Transit Series">RTS</a> model were sold to <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Manufacturing_Corporation" title="Transportation Manufacturing Corporation">Transportation Manufacturing Corporation</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Motor_Coach_Industries" title="Motor Coach Industries">Motor Coach Industries</a> of Canada purchased the <a href="/wiki/Classic_(transit_bus)" title="Classic (transit bus)">Classic</a> design.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1998, GMC's official branding on vehicles was shortened from "GMC Truck" to simply "GMC".</p>
<p>In 2002, GMC released a book entitled, <i>GMC: The First 100 Years</i>, a complete history of the company.</p>
<p>GMC currently manufactures <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">SUVs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup trucks</a>, vans, light-duty trucks, and <a href="/wiki/Truck_classification#Medium_duty" title="Truck classification">medium duty trucks</a>. In the past, GMC also produced <a href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire apparatus">fire trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">ambulances</a>, heavy-duty trucks, <a href="/wiki/Military_vehicle" title="Military vehicle">military vehicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorhome" title="Motorhome">motorhomes</a>, and transit <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a>.</p>

<p>Starting in 1920, GMC and Chevrolet trucks are virtually identical except for the grilles and nameplates, though their differences have varied over the years. From 1955 through 1959 small (less than 2 ton) GMC trucks with gasoline V8s were equipped with Pontiac, Buick, and Oldsmobile V8s (the Canadian trucks used Chevrolet engines). GMC had its own line of inline 6 cylinder engines, known as "Jimmy's" from 1939-1959, and their own <a href="/wiki/GMC_V6_engine" title="GMC V6 engine">V6</a> from 1960-1974(also a V8, V12). Chevrolet trucks were marketed towards private ownership, while GMC was focused towards commercial uses.</p>
<p>New Chevrolet vehicles are sold exclusively at Chevrolet dealerships, GMC <a href="/wiki/Light_truck" title="Light truck">light trucks</a> have recently been made available to Buick and Cadillac dealerships with previous Pontiac and Oldsmobile dealerships also having similar arrangements, and separate franchises exist for medium and light-duty commercial models as well. This crossover allowed GM dealers that did not sell Chevrolets to offer full lineups of both cars and trucks by offering GMC's trucks alongside "non-truck" divisions. Between 1962 and 1972, most GMC vehicles were equipped with quad-headlights, while their Chevrolet clones were equipped with dual-headlights.</p>
<p>In 1971, GMC marketed their version of the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_El_Camino" title="Chevrolet El Camino">Chevrolet El Camino</a>, which was based on the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Chevelle" title="Chevrolet Chevelle">Chevrolet Chevelle</a>. Called Sprint, it was virtually identical to the El Camino, and a sport version, the SP, was equivalent to the El Camino SS. In 1973, with GM’s introduction of the new "rounded line" series trucks, GMC and Chevrolet trucks became even more similar, ending production of GMC’s quad-headlight models, and setting the standard for the Chevrolet/GMC line of trucks for over thirty years. During this period, the companies' sister models (<a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Silverado" title="Chevrolet Silverado">Silverado</a>/<a href="/wiki/GMC_Sierra" class="mw-redirect" title="GMC Sierra">Sierra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_K5_Blazer" title="Chevrolet K5 Blazer">Blazer</a>/<a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_K5_Blazer" title="Chevrolet K5 Blazer">Jimmy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Tahoe" title="Chevrolet Tahoe">Tahoe</a>/<a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Tahoe" title="Chevrolet Tahoe">Yukon</a>, etc.) shared everything except for trims and prices. GM has recently begun a divergence in design between the two lines with the 2007 model <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Silverado" title="Chevrolet Silverado">Silverados</a> and <a href="/wiki/GMC_Sierra" class="mw-redirect" title="GMC Sierra">Sierras</a>, which have some differences in sheet metal and style.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1996, GM merged GMC with the <a href="/wiki/Pontiac" title="Pontiac">Pontiac</a> division in order to give Pontiac dealerships a line of trucks mainly to allow Pontiac dealers to compete with <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">Chevrolet</a>, which offered a full lineup of vehicles. While many GMC and Chevrolet trucks are mechanically identical, GMC is positioned as a premium offering to the mainstream Chevrolet brand, with luxury vehicles such as the <a href="/wiki/GMC_Denali" title="GMC Denali">Denali series</a>. The profitability of the GMC brand helped its survival in 2009 during the <a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Chapter_11_reorganization" title="General Motors Chapter 11 reorganization">General Motors Chapter 11 reorganization</a>, and after the discontinuation of the Pontiac brand, many <a href="/wiki/Buick" title="Buick">Buick</a> franchises also sell GMC light-duty vehicles in the United States and Canada.</p>
<p>In 2007, GMC introduced the <a href="/wiki/GMC_Acadia" title="GMC Acadia">Acadia</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Crossover_SUV" class="mw-redirect" title="Crossover SUV">crossover SUV</a>, which was the division's first <a href="/wiki/Unibody" class="mw-redirect" title="Unibody">unibody</a> vehicle whose predecessor, the GMT-360 based <a href="/wiki/GMC_Envoy" title="GMC Envoy">Envoy</a>, was discontinued with the closure of GM's <a href="/wiki/Moraine_Assembly#Plant_closure" title="Moraine Assembly">Moraine, Ohio plant</a> on December 23, 2008.</p>
<p>In 2009, GMC introduced the <a href="/wiki/GMC_Terrain" title="GMC Terrain">Terrain</a>, a mid-size crossover SUV based on GM's Theta platform which slots below the Acadia as GMC's smallest crossover, replacing the <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Torrent" title="Pontiac Torrent">Pontiac Torrent</a> and sharing no sheetmetal with the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Equinox" title="Chevrolet Equinox">Chevrolet Equinox</a>.</p>
