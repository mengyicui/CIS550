<p><b>Automobile Dacia S.A.</b><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (<small>Romanian pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Romanian" title="Help:IPA for Romanian">[ˈdat͡ʃi.a]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<a href="/wiki/File:Ro-Dacia.ogg" title="File:Ro-Dacia.ogg"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" width="13" height="13" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="500" /></a> <a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f2/Ro-Dacia.ogg" class="internal" title="Ro-Dacia.ogg">listen</a>)</small>) is a Romanian <a href="/wiki/List_of_automobile_manufacturers" title="List of automobile manufacturers">car manufacturer</a> that takes its name from <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">the historic region</a> that constitutes much of present-day Romania. The company was founded in 1966, and has been a subsidiary of the French car manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> since 1999. It is Romania's top company by revenue<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the largest exporter, constituting 7.3% of the country's total exports in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Dacia manufacturing plant in <a href="/wiki/Mioveni" title="Mioveni">Mioveni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arge%C8%99_County" title="Argeș County">Argeș County</a> is Europe's fifth biggest car manufacturing facility by volume produced with 584,219 units (cars and <a href="/wiki/Knock-down_kit" title="Knock-down kit">CKD kits</a>) delivered in 2016<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and 550,912 units in 2015.</p>
<p>From the 2008–09 season, Dacia was the sponsor of <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> football club <a href="/wiki/Udinese_Calcio" title="Udinese Calcio">Udinese Calcio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stadio_Friuli" title="Stadio Friuli">Stadio Friuli</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile_Dacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dacia automobile company was founded in 1966 under the name <b>Uzina de Autoturisme Pitești (UAP)</b>. The main Dacia factory was built in 1968, in Colibași (now called <a href="/wiki/Mioveni" title="Mioveni">Mioveni</a>), near <a href="/wiki/Pite%C8%99ti" title="Pitești">Pitești</a>. Dacia acquired the tooling and basic designs of the <a href="/wiki/Renault_12" title="Renault 12">Renault 12</a>. However, until the tooling was ready it was decided to produce the <a href="/wiki/Renault_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault 8">Renault 8</a> under licence; it was known as the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_1100" class="mw-redirect" title="Dacia 1100">Dacia 1100</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> From 1968 to 1972, 37,546 cars of model 1100 were produced,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> with a very minor cosmetic change to the front in early 1970. Also produced in very limited numbers was the <i>1100S</i>, with twin headlamps and a more powerful engine, used by the police and in motor racing.</p>

<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Dacia_1300" title="Dacia 1300">Dacia 1300</a> left the assembly line ready for 23 August parade in 1969, and was exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> shows of that year. Romanians were delighted with the modernity and reliability of the car, and waiting lists were always lengthy. By 1970, there were two variants: the standard <i>1300</i> and the <i>1300L</i> (for Lux); in 1974 the <i>1301 Lux Super</i> was introduced, which had novelties such as a heated rear screen, a radio, windscreen mirrors on both sides and a more luxurious trim. This was reserved for the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Communist_Party" title="Romanian Communist Party">Communist Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Nomenklatura" title="Nomenklatura">nomenklatura</a>.<br />
Changes soon followed as export markets opened up. In 1973, the estate variant, <i>1300 Break</i>, was produced. There were <i>1300F</i> (estate with no rear seats, for carrying goods) and <i>1300S</i> (ambulance) variants, and in 1975 the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_1302" class="mw-redirect" title="Dacia 1302">Dacia 1302</a> pick-up was developed. 2,000 units were made until 1982.</p>
<p>Dacia also produced the <i>Estafette</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Complete_knock_down" class="mw-redirect" title="Complete knock down">CKD</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Estafette" title="Renault Estafette">Renault Estafette</a> van, in limited numbers, but given the competition of the Bucharest-made T.V. van, numbers were very limited. In the very early 1980s, the <a href="/wiki/Renault_20" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault 20">Renault 20</a> was also assembled as the <i><a href="/wiki/Dacia_2000" class="mw-redirect" title="Dacia 2000">Dacia 2000</a></i>; because of the exclusivity of this model numbers were always very limited. The <i>2000</i> was only available in dark blue or black, and was reserved for the Party elite. In 1978 plans were revealed for the <a href="/wiki/Renault_18" title="Renault 18">Renault 18</a> to be assembled by Dacia, but the Renault contract lapsed and Dacia went its own way.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the Bucharest show in 1979, the restyled <a href="/wiki/Dacia_1310" class="mw-redirect" title="Dacia 1310">1310</a> models were presented. These had quad lamps at the front, larger lamps at the rear, re-profiled bumpers, and a new interior. The changes were heavily inspired by Renault's own restyling of its 12 in 1975. After a brief series of "crossover" cars in 1981 (for example, there were no more rectangular headlights available for the <i>1300</i>, so the last models used the quad lamps of the <i>1310</i>), the <i>1310</i> hit the Romanian market in late 1981. In the UK, where it was known as the <b>Dacia Denem</b>, the top of the range model included such luxuries as a five-speed gearbox, alloy wheels and electric windows. The advertising slogan used for the car was "The Very Acceptable Dacia Denem". Sales were very limited, and the number surviving are not thought to exceed single digit figures, although the Romanian Embassy in <a href="/wiki/South_Kensington" title="South Kensington">South Kensington</a> kept a fleet running until the mid-1990s. Sales of the pick-up version, known as the <i>Shifter</i>, continued until the 1990s, and the <a href="/wiki/ARO_10" title="ARO 10">Aro 10</a> was also sold as the <i><a href="/wiki/Dacia_Duster" title="Dacia Duster">Dacia Duster</a></i>. The plug was pulled on the Denem, however, in late 1982.</p>
<p>At the same time the <i>Sport</i> model was produced.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> At the 1980 Bucharest international trade fair, crowds admired the <i><a href="/wiki/File:Dacia_brasovia-1.jpg" title="File:Dacia brasovia-1.jpg">Braşovia</a></i>, a prototype of a sports <a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">coupé</a> based on the <i>1310</i> and developed at a service station in <a href="/wiki/Bra%C5%9Fov" class="mw-redirect" title="Braşov">Braşov</a>. The go-ahead was given for a prestige model, and so from 1983 the two-door Dacia <i>1310 Sport</i> (and later the <i>1410 Sport</i>), was available for general consumption. These were very popular for rallies, and racing drivers such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicu_Grigora%C5%9F&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nicu Grigoraş (page does not exist)">Nicu Grigoraş</a> tuned them to extract extraordinary power from the old Renault engine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1980s">The 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile_Dacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The designers were still coming up with fresh ideas, many of them shrouded in secrecy. Prototypes such as the 500cc Mini-Dacia, as well as Dacia <i>1310</i> variants, were designed; some, such as the Dacia <i>1310 Limousine</i>, are still on the road. These cars are eagerly prized by Dacia enthusiasts, and Dacia web forums are full of evidence about the rarities and oddities produced by Dacia during the 1980s. In 1982, after the <i>1302</i> was dropped, the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Pick-Up" title="Dacia Pick-Up">Dacia <i>1304</i></a> Pick-up and Drop-side models were introduced. Actually, they had been launched from 1981. These were a commercial success and remained in production, gradually being modified along with the rest of the range, until December 2006. From 1985, also, the <i>1410</i> was available as a larger-engined variant, while the relatively short-lived <i>1210</i> was the economy variant until about 1992.</p>

<p>For the 1984 model year, the de luxe MS and MLS models presented slight modifications on the 1310 platform, with a new spoiler and chunkier, rubber mouldings around the front grille and headlamps. In 1985, the rear vent was changed from a narrow horizontal aperture to a rhomboid aperture, much like that of the Renault 12 phase 2. From late 1985 all models had the large rubber mouldings around the front grille and headlamps, plus a spoiler: the line-up was changed to TX and TLX, though other designations were occasionally seen. A five-speed gearbox also became an option. Models destined for Canada (initially called GTL) sported twin fuel tanks. In parallel, work was developing on the <i>1320 CN1</i> model, which was a hatchback based on the <i>1310</i>. The new front end seen on the <i>1320</i> also appeared on the top-of-the-range and special-order models from about 1986; these cars were distinguishable by two large headlights, a much plusher interior clad in blue plastic and a new dashboard known as the CN1, and - occasionally - faired-in door handles. Many were owned by senior officials in the Communist Central Committee. While the <i>1320</i> was the most expensive model in the Dacia range when it appeared in 1988, many were used as taxis. The <i>1320</i> did not last long, however; as early as 1989 there were prototypes using the front of the <i>1320</i> and a new rear, with wrap-around tail lights and other (relatively) modern features.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1990s">The 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile_Dacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>1320</i> model emerged in 1991 as the Dacia <i>1325 Liberta</i> (after the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Revolution_of_1989" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanian Revolution of 1989">1989 revolution</a>, themes of liberty were very much in fashion) and stayed in production until 1996.<br />
The last of the quad-lamped models were produced in 1992, and all the Dacia range received the new front end of the <i>1320</i>, called CN1. An effort was made to rejuvenate the model range: the Sport was dropped, due to lack of sales, and new commercial vehicles were introduced. The <i>1307</i> was a double-cabbed pick-up; the <i>1309</i> was an estate with a tarpaulin instead of a boot; the car was a cross-over between estate and pick-up and sold very well on the Chinese market. There were also several prototypes, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <i>1610</i> diesel estate, powered by <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>, with about 150 units sold</li>
<li>the Dacia <i>Star</i>, with curved side windows</li>
<li>the <i>1308 Jumbo</i>, a camper-van, and several attempts to give the <i>1310</i> a more modern look by grafting the front of contemporary cars such as the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Primera" title="Nissan Primera">Nissan Primera</a> onto it.</li>
</ul>
<p>The cars of 1992 to 1994 are curiosities: although efforts were obviously being made to renew the model range, there were numerous stylistic hangovers from the quad-lamped models. Thus, the last of the 1983-designed dashboards were seen in 1994 although a new dashboard had been seen on some model ranges since 1987. Similarly, although the CN1 restyling eliminated anachronisms such as a kink upwards at the C-pillar and a rubber rear spoiler, it was not applied consistently.</p>
<p>The 1994 facelift was known within the industry as CN2. A reprojected front end was distinguished by a horizontal metal line in the grille. There were new headlamps, a new radiator grille, and front and rear bumpers. Inside, there was a new dashboard for the base models, while the top-of-the-range cars had luxuries such as body-coloured bumpers, rear head restraints, a radio-cassette, hubcaps, and the ever-present CN1 dashboard, this time in black plastic. This model was not to stay in production for very long; in 1995 the CN3 type was introduced. Practically the only differences were those of trim level and the radiator grille.</p>

<p>From late 1994 little attention was paid to the improvement of the 1310 range, as Dacia launched a new model, the <i><a href="/wiki/Dacia_Nova" title="Dacia Nova">Dacia Nova</a></i>. This was a compact saloon/sedan or hatchback with a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">three-box design</a>. The design was rather outdated, because development work had started in 1983. The <i>Nova</i> is a 100% Romanian design<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, started after the end of any French involvement in Dacia<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The model was unpopular, due to reliability and rustproofing issues. However, after improvements in 1996, the <i>Nova</i> became more often seen on Romanian roads. In 1998 a seven-seater prototype was produced using the panels and windows from the standard <i>Nova</i> saloon.</p>

<p>In 1998, the anniversary year of three decades of production since the first Dacia rolled off the assembly line, vehicle number 2,000,000 emerged from the plant; this year saw the last restyling of the 1310. It was known as CN4 and involved a comprehensive restyling of the front end, new door handles, and a lightly restyled rear view mirror. The estate version was fitted with larger tail lights. Even though the model was over thirty years old, it sold exceptionally well due to a starting price of about 4,200 <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euros</a> and high availability of parts. "Goodies" such as a fuel-injection also helped keep the model relatively modern.</p>
<p>In 1999, a special edition was produced for the first time, known as Dacia <i>Dedicaţie</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This luxury version of the saloon and estate had alloy wheels, body-coloured bumpers, power steering, electric windows, and a far better level of finish. The models were all painted two-tone silver, and sold at a significantly higher price. From 2002, the cars became known as Berlina and Break, with the 1310 lettering being relegated to an insignificant position underneath the side light.</p>
<p>On 21 July 2004, the last models of the 1300 series rolled out the gates of the <a href="/wiki/Mioveni" title="Mioveni">Mioveni</a> production facility, one month before their 35th anniversary. The last Dacia 1310 (saloon version), number 1,979,730, will be kept in the Dacia Museum.</p>

<p>On 8 December 2006, the utility <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Pick-Up" title="Dacia Pick-Up">Dacia Pick-Up</a> suffered the same fate. Although many improvements had been made in recent years, such as four-wheel drive, the introduction of a 1.9 diesel engine, the dashboard from the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Solenza" title="Dacia Solenza">Dacia Solenza</a> (also seen on the very last 1310s) and wheels fastened by five studs as opposed to three, Romania's entry to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> effectively prevented the continued production of the old models. The assembly lines will be remodelled and expanded to increase production of the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Logan" title="Dacia Logan">Dacia Logan</a>.</p>
<p>In over 34 years of production, and more than 2.5 million units produced, the Dacia <i>1300/1310</i> easily became the most common car on Romanian roads. Almost everybody owned one and became adept at carrying out repairs or home-made modifications. For example, many older cars had newer front ends grafted onto them to make them seem more modern, or purely because newer parts were easier to get. Consequently, original early 1300s are quite rare, with prices steadily increasing for the best-preserved models. Tuning of Dacias is also a popular pastime, although the home-made nature of much of the work casts doubt on the level of quality, safety and reliability of the finished product.</p>
<p>According to popular belief, during the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Romania">Communist era</a>, in the plant where Dacias were made, there were two assembly lines: one line producing Dacias intended for sale in Romania and the other line producing the same car (albeit from superior parts, and assembled with greater care) for export. Romanians living near the border would commonly purchase their Dacia in neighbouring countries expecting a higher level of quality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Meanwhile, work was continuing on the other Dacia models. In September 1999, Dacia was bought by the Renault group, with a view to making Romania its hub of automobile development in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, and investment was consequently increased. The first sign of this came in 2000, with the introduction of the <i><a href="/wiki/Dacia_SuperNova" class="mw-redirect" title="Dacia SuperNova">SupeRNova</a></i>, an improved version of the <i>Nova</i> with engine and transmission from Renault. The top-of-the range version had air conditioning, electric windows and a CD player. Sales were very good, although the outdatedness of the concept was striking. Dacia sold 53,000 vehicles in 2002 and it holds an almost 50 per cent market share in Romania.</p>
<p>In 2003, a restyled version replaced the <i>SupeRNova</i> with <i><a href="/wiki/Dacia_Solenza" title="Dacia Solenza">Dacia Solenza</a></i>, featuring a new interior, the options for an airbag. This was, however, only meant to be a stopgap model filling the need for a saloon model before the introduction of the all-important <i><a href="/wiki/Dacia_Logan" title="Dacia Logan">Dacia Logan</a></i>, as well as to familiarise workers with the demands of manufacturing a model acceptable to <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western European</a> markets. Production was stopped in 2005.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Logan" title="Dacia Logan">Dacia Logan</a> is the most successful model since the original <i>1300</i>. It was introduced after considerable media interest in August 2004, and despite design-related criticism, it became one of the top-selling cars in Central and Eastern Europe, as well as Russia. The Logan is sold in many countries, occasionally under the Renault brand. It was awarded 3 (out of 5) stars in <a href="/wiki/NCAP_crash_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="NCAP crash testing">NCAP crash testing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It remains by far the best-selling car in Romania, comprising a 43% share of Dacia's total Romanian sales in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A diesel version was also introduced in 2005. Before its launch, it was known as the 5000-Euro car due to its projected launch price. This was never quite the case, although it is one of the cheapest cars for its size on the market.</p>
<p>In 2006, the prototype Logan Steppe was exhibited. This showed a speculative 4x4 estate version of the Logan. The estate version was launched in late 2006. The van, basically an estate with the rear windows filled in and a separate cabin for the driver, was launched in February 2007 after the stopping production of the classic utility vehicle, the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Pick-Up" title="Dacia Pick-Up">Dacia Pick-Up</a>.</p>
<p>The Renault Technologie Roumanie engineering centre was set up in 2006, in <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a>, Romania. Employing approximately 2,500 engineers, its main fields of activity are the development, testing and design of the new vehicles in the Dacia range, as well as the marketing and technical support.<sup id="cite_ref-rtr_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rtr-15">[15]</a></sup> The following year, the company also set up a styling office, Renault Design Central Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-monde_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monde-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>A pick-up model and the Logan-based hatchback <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Sandero" title="Dacia Sandero">Sandero</a> were launched in 2008, the latter at the Geneva Motor Show.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This also marked a point of rebranding for the company,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> which adopted a new logo and later the same year launched the facelifted Logan model.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, a new concept called the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Duster" title="Dacia Duster">Dacia Duster</a> was unveiled at the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Motor_Show" title="Geneva Motor Show">Geneva Motor Show</a>, signaling manufacturer's intentions of releasing an SUV model.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This was reportedly the inauguration of the platform that would be used for the second generation of the Logan and Sandero models, released in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, the Dacia Duster was exhibited at the Geneva Motor Show. It is the first crossover <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">SUV</a> built by Dacia since the Renault acquisition, in 4x2 and 4x4 versions. It was revealed on 8 December 2009, and became available in Europe on 18 March 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2010, a testing center was opened in <a href="/wiki/Titu" title="Titu">Titu</a>, Romania, as part of Renault Technologie Roumanie.<sup id="cite_ref-titu_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-titu-24">[24]</a></sup> It is intended for developing and optimizing the new vehicles in the Dacia range and has nine types of track with a total length of 32 kilometres and around 100 test benches, used to test the resistance of vehicles and replacement parts to cold, heat and rain.<sup id="cite_ref-monde_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monde-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>In September 2012, the second generations of the Logan and Sandero were revealed at the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Motor_Show" title="Paris Motor Show">Paris Motor Show</a>. They feature a common front end design and come with improved standard safety features, a new engine and other new comfort equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following year, the facelifted Duster was presented at the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Motor_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt Motor Show">Frankfurt Motor Show</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It received mild modifications to the front and rear ends, the new interior introduced on the new Logan and Sandero models,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> as well as a new turbocharged petrol engine.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile_Dacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The company's single plant is located in <a href="/wiki/Mioveni" title="Mioveni">Mioveni</a>, Romania, together with its headquarters, and has a production capacity of 350,000 vehicles per year.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It is divided into several sections, such as bodywork, painting, assembly, mechanical and chassis, foundry etc.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It works in conjunction with the Renault Technologie Roumanie engineering centre, located in <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> and set up in 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-rtr_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rtr-15">[15]</a></sup> which also comprises a styling office, Renault Design Central Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-monde_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monde-16">[16]</a></sup> and with the testing center located in <a href="/wiki/Titu" title="Titu">Titu</a>, opened in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-titu_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-titu-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>A large logistical centre was also set near the plant in March 2005, the International Logistic Network, from where <a href="/wiki/Complete_knock_down" class="mw-redirect" title="Complete knock down">complete knock down</a> (CKD) kits are exported to other Renault production sites in Russia, Morocco, Colombia, Argentina, Brazil, South Africa, Iran and India.<sup id="cite_ref-logistica_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-logistica-33">[33]</a></sup> It is reportedly the biggest logistic centre of its kind not only in the Renault Group, but in the entire world automotive industry.<sup id="cite_ref-project_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-project-34">[34]</a></sup> In 2012, the total equivalent of the kits sent worldwide from the centre was 920,646 vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-logistica_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-logistica-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, two of the Dacia models, the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Lodgy" title="Dacia Lodgy">Lodgy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dacia_Dokker" title="Dacia Dokker">Dokker</a> vans, are manufactured exclusively at the Renault factory in <a href="/wiki/Tangier" title="Tangier">Tangier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kit_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kit-35">[35]</a></sup> The Logan and the Sandero are also manufactured in Casablanca, Morocco, the latter in Tangier too.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sales">Sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile_Dacia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dacia brand is marketed in most of the Western and Eastern European countries, as well as in some Northern African countries,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> such as Algeria, Tunisia or Morocco. Its models have also been produced by Renault in its production sites on other continents (in Russia, Iran, India, South Africa, Colombia and Brazil) and sold under its own brand or other specific local brands (such as <a href="/wiki/Mahindra_%26_Mahindra" title="Mahindra &amp; Mahindra">Mahindra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lada" title="Lada">Lada</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a> for example<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>).</p>
<p>In 2012, Dacia sold a total of 359,822 vehicles, of which approximately 230,000 were exported to Western Europe, the most of them in France and in Germany. Besides the domestic market, its other key markets have been Algeria, Turkey, Italy and Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>For 2014 the company accounted for 511,465 vehicles sold in 43 different countries thus breaking another record and exceeding the 500,000 threshold.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> On 6 May 2014, the main Dacia plant <a href="/wiki/Mioveni" title="Mioveni">Mioveni</a> produced its 5 millionth car, so approximately the 3 millionth car since Renault drove the brand.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 3.5 millionth Dacia car, since the Dacia brand revival ten years before, was sold in the UK in November 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, the company has set a new sales record, with a total of 550,920 units marketed.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
